About

May 1 is International Worker's Day is also known as May Day. This day is dedicated to recognising the contributions and achievements of workers worldwide and promoting workers’ rights and opportunities.

History

On May 1st, 1886, labour unions in the United States initiated a strike advocating for an eight-hour workday, a pivotal moment in labour history. The strike culminated in the tragic events of May 4th, 1886, at Chicago’s Haymarket Square, where a peaceful rally turned violent with a bomb explosion, resulting in casualties among both civilians and police officers.

Following a meeting in Paris in 1889, the decision to commemorate May Day annually was made, inspired by the Chicago protests. Raymond Lavigne’s proposal for international demonstrations to mark the anniversary of the Haymarket events laid the foundation for the official recognition of May Day as an annual event by the International’s 2nd Congress in 1891.

In India, on May 1st, 1923, where the first May Day celebrations were organised in Chennai by the Labour Kisan Party of Hindustan, led by Comrade Singaravelar, who arranged two significant meetings to honour this occasion.

Significance

  • Labour Day is celebrated with various events and festivities across the globe. From parades and marches to speeches and cultural performances, these celebrations serve to honour the contributions of workers and the labour movement.
  • In many countries, Labour Day is observed as a national public holiday, allowing workers to take a well-deserved break and reflect on the progress made in securing their rights and improving their working conditions.
  • This annual observance is a powerful reminder of the ongoing struggle for social justice and the crucial role that workers play in the fabric of our societies.
  • By recognising the hard work and sacrifices of the labour force, we can collectively work towards a future where decent work and social justice are the cornerstones of a thriving global economy.
